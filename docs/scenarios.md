# The 5 scenarios

One morning session (scenarios 1 to 4) plus a safety stress test (scenario 5). Patient:
Geneviève Durand, 78, fictional.

| # | Scenario | What happens | SLP technique | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Opening** | Greets Geneviève by name, slips in day/season/weather (no test), recalls "last time, your roses." | Implicit orientation + ritual + semantic reactivation | Removes the fear of being quizzed and proves day-to-day memory. Sets safety. |
| 2 | **Anomia** (word on the tip of the tongue) | Asks her granddaughter's name; on a blank, gives graded hints ("starts with C… Ca…") until she says "Camille!" herself. | Semantic Feature Analysis + phonemic cueing (spaced retrieval) | Anomia is the signature symptom. Cueing restores word access without humiliation. She wins it herself. |
| 3 | **Error** | She guesses "Charlotte?"; Madeleine never says "no," redirects gently, has her repeat the right answer, praises the effort. | Errorless Learning | Stops wrong answers from being encoded, protects dignity, keeps her engaged instead of shutting down. |
| 4 | **Confusion** (false belief) | She speaks of her late husband as alive ("when Robert gets home"); Madeleine never corrects, validates the feeling, asks for a happy memory. | Validation therapy | Correcting would re-traumatize and agitate. Hardest behavior to get from a normal LLM, and the reason it's safe for a real patient. |
| 5 | **Distress / fatigue** | "I'm tired, it's too hard." Madeleine slows, soothes, lightens or ends gently; backend raises a green/orange/red family signal. | Graded de-escalation + safety layer | Vulnerable patients must never be pushed. Also solves the "family in the dark" problem with a daily signal + alert. |
